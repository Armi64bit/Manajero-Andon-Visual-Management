Concrete Experience (Why)
Why use the Andon visual management method?

Problem Identification: Andon systems quickly identify problems in the production process. By providing immediate visual or audible alerts, they enable quick intervention to prevent defects and minimize downtime.

Quality Assurance: Ensuring high-quality production standards by enabling immediate corrective actions when deviations occur.

Efficiency: Streamlining communication and responses to issues, leading to more efficient operations.

Employee Empowerment: Workers on the production line can stop the production process if they notice a problem, fostering a culture of accountability and continuous improvement.

Reflective Observation (What)
What is the Andon visual management method?

Visual Signals: Andon systems typically use lights, displays, or other visual signals to indicate the status of the production process. Common indicators include green for normal operations, yellow for a caution or minor issue, and red for a significant problem that needs immediate attention.

Real-Time Alerts: Provides real-time alerts to supervisors and workers about the status of production processes.

Problem Resolution: Alerts prompt immediate action to resolve issues, often involving team leaders or maintenance personnel.

Integration: Can be integrated with other systems like PLCs (Programmable Logic Controllers) for automated monitoring and reporting.

First step

1. Define the Purpose and Scope

Determine what processes or areas will benefit from Andon visual management. Identify key metrics or parameters you want to monitor.
Define clear objectives and metrics to measure success.

Second step

2. Select Visual Indicators

Choose visual indicators (lights, boards, displays) to represent the status of operations:

  • Lights: Green for normal, yellow for attention required, red for stoppage.
  • Boards: Physical or digital displays showing real-time data.

3. Design Andon Board Layout

Create the layout for your Andon board:

  • Sections: Divide into areas if monitoring multiple processes.
  • Labels: Clearly label each section or indicator.
  • Information Display: Decide what to display (e.g., process status, time since last incident).

4. Choose Andon Trigger Mechanisms

Determine what events or conditions will trigger an Andon alert:

  • Manual: Operators press a button or pull a cord to indicate an issue.
  • Automated: Sensors detect abnormalities in production metrics (e.g., downtime, error rates).

5. Implement Visual Controls

Install and integrate the chosen visual indicators with your workspace:

  • Lights: Mount Andon lights where they are easily visible to all relevant personnel.
  • Boards: Set up digital displays or physical boards in strategic locations.

6. Train Personnel

Educate employees on the Andon system:

  • Operation: How to trigger an alert, interpret indicators, and respond appropriately.
  • Responsibilities: Define roles and responsibilities for different levels of alerts.

7. Establish Response Procedures

Develop clear protocols for responding to Andon alerts:

  • Escalation: Define who to notify for each level of alert (e.g., team leader, maintenance).
  • Resolution: Standard procedures for addressing and resolving issues promptly.

8. Monitor and Improve

Continuously monitor the effectiveness of your Andon system:

  • Feedback: Solicit feedback from employees on system usability and effectiveness.
  • Metrics: Track key performance indicators (KPIs) related to response times and issue resolution.

9. Iterate and Optimize

Based on feedback and data, make iterative improvements to your Andon system:

  • Adjustments: Modify triggers or indicators as processes evolve.
  • Training: Provide ongoing training to ensure all personnel are proficient with the system.

10. Integrate with Continuous Improvement

Incorporate Andon insights into your continuous improvement processes:

  • Root Cause Analysis: Use Andon data to identify recurring issues and implement permanent solutions.
  • Kaizen: Encourage a culture of continuous improvement based on Andon system feedback.

Active Experimentation (What If)
What if we implement the Andon visual management method? Examples and scenarios

Scenario 1: Early Defect Detection
Example: A worker notices a defect in a part being assembled. They activate the Andon system, triggering a yellow light. The team leader immediately comes to assess the situation, identifies the root cause, and fixes the issue before it affects the entire batch.
Outcome: Early detection prevents a large number of defective products, saving costs and maintaining quality standards.

Scenario 2: Equipment Malfunction
Example: A machine starts malfunctioning, producing irregular outputs. The worker pulls the Andon cord, activating a red light and halting production. Maintenance personnel are alerted and quickly repair the machine.
Outcome: Quick intervention minimizes downtime and prevents further damage to the equipment.

Scenario 3: Continuous Improvement
Example: Over a month, data from the Andon system reveals that a specific machine frequently triggers alerts. Analysis shows that it is due for a major maintenance overhaul.
Outcome: Proactive maintenance scheduling prevents future breakdowns, improving overall efficiency and reliability.
