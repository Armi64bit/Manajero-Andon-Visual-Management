Concrete Experience (Why)
Why use the Andon visual management method?

Problem Identification: Andon systems quickly identify problems in the production process by providing immediate visual or audible alerts. Strategically placed at key points along the production line, these systems enable quick intervention to prevent defects and minimize downtime. When an issue is detected, the Andon system signals the appropriate personnel to take corrective action, maintaining production flow and addressing disruptions swiftly. Additionally, the data collected can be analyzed to identify recurring issues and improve processes, enhancing our ability to uphold high production standards.

Quality Assurance: Ensuring high-quality production standards by enabling immediate corrective actions when deviations occur. This involves regular inspections, continuous monitoring of production processes, and the implementation of robust quality control measures. By identifying potential issues early and addressing them promptly, we maintain product consistency and meet customer expectations. Our commitment to quality assurance also includes training staff on best practices and utilizing advanced technologies to detect and rectify any discrepancies in real-time.

Efficiency: Streamlining communication and responses to issues, leading to more efficient operations. By optimizing workflows and utilizing advanced communication tools, we ensure that information is transmitted quickly and accurately across all departments. This reduces downtime and accelerates decision-making processes. Additionally, implementing automated systems for routine tasks minimizes human error and allows employees to focus on more critical activities. As a result, we achieve higher productivity, faster issue resolution, and an overall improvement in operational efficiency.

Employee Empowerment: Workers on the production line can stop the production process if they notice a problem, fostering a culture of accountability and continuous improvement. By granting employees the authority to halt production, we empower them to take immediate action when issues arise, ensuring that defects do not progress further down the line. This approach not only prevents potential quality issues but also encourages employees to take ownership of their work. Regular training and open communication channels support this empowerment, providing workers with the knowledge and confidence to identify and address problems effectively. This commitment to employee empowerment leads to higher job satisfaction, increased vigilance, and a proactive approach to maintaining high production standards.

Reflective Observation (What)
What is the Andon visual management method?

Visual Signals: Andon systems typically use lights, displays, or other visual signals to indicate the status of the production process. Common indicators include green for normal operations, yellow for a caution or minor issue, and red for a significant problem that needs immediate attention.

Real-Time Alerts: Provides real-time alerts to supervisors and workers about the status of production processes. These alerts, delivered through visual signals, audible alarms, or digital notifications, ensure that any issues are immediately brought to the attention of relevant personnel. This rapid communication enables quick decision-making and prompt corrective actions, minimizing the impact of potential problems on production efficiency. Real-time alerts also facilitate better coordination among team members, as everyone is instantly informed of the situation. By keeping all stakeholders updated, we enhance our ability to maintain smooth operations, prevent downtime, and ensure that production targets are met consistently.

Problem Resolution: Alerts prompt immediate action to resolve issues, often involving team leaders or maintenance personnel. This proactive approach ensures that potential problems are addressed swiftly, minimizing downtime and optimizing operational efficiency. Team leaders are notified promptly, allowing them to mobilize resources and coordinate efforts effectively. Maintenance personnel are deployed promptly to investigate and resolve issues, utilizing their expertise to implement lasting solutions and prevent recurrence.

Integration: The system can seamlessly integrate with other critical systems such as PLCs (Programmable Logic Controllers) for automated monitoring and reporting. This integration enhances efficiency by enabling real-time data exchange and synchronized operations across multiple platforms. By interfacing with PLCs, the system can retrieve sensor data directly from machinery and equipment, facilitating comprehensive monitoring and proactive maintenance. This integrated approach not only streamlines operations but also enhances decision-making capabilities through consolidated data analysis and automated reporting, ensuring optimal performance and reliability.

 Second step

2. Select Visual Indicators

Choose visual indicators (lights, boards, displays) to represent the status of operations. These indicators should be easily visible and understandable by all team members to ensure quick and accurate communication of the current status:

  • Lights: Use color-coded lights to indicate different statuses. For example:
    • Green: Normal operation
    • Yellow: Attention required
    • Red: Stoppage
    These lights can be placed at strategic locations to ensure visibility.
  • Boards: Physical or digital displays showing real-time data. These boards can include:
    • Current production status
    • Key performance indicators (KPIs)
    • Alerts and notifications
    Digital boards can be updated automatically through integrated systems, while physical boards might require manual updates.

Selecting the right visual indicators is crucial for maintaining an efficient and responsive operation. Ensure that the indicators are standardized and clearly understood by all team members.

3. Design Andon Board Layout

Create the layout for your Andon board. A well-organized Andon board is crucial for quick comprehension and effective decision-making:

Sections: Divide the board into distinct sections if you need to monitor multiple processes or areas. For example:
  • Production lines
  • Maintenance activities
  • Quality control
This helps in quickly identifying the area that requires attention.
Labels: Clearly label each section or indicator to avoid confusion. Use large, readable fonts and consider color-coding labels for additional clarity.
  • Process names
  • Machine IDs
  • Shift timings
Information Display: Decide what critical information you need to display on the Andon board. This could include:
  • Current process status (running, stopped, maintenance)
  • Time since the last incident or downtime
  • Performance metrics (e.g., output rate, defect rate)
Ensure the information is updated in real-time to provide accurate and timely data for decision-making.

A well-designed Andon board can significantly improve communication and efficiency on the shop floor. Regularly review and update the layout and content to ensure it continues to meet the needs of your operations.

4. Choose Andon Trigger Mechanisms

Determine what events or conditions will trigger an Andon alert. Identifying the right triggers is essential for ensuring timely intervention and maintaining smooth operations:

    Manual: Operators can manually trigger an Andon alert by pressing a button or pulling a cord. This method allows for immediate human intervention when an issue is detected. Examples include:
    • Identifying a defect in the production line
    • Noticing a safety hazard
    • Requesting assistance or support
    Manual triggers rely on the vigilance and prompt response of operators.
    Automated: Automated systems use sensors and software to detect abnormalities in production metrics and trigger Andon alerts without human intervention. This can help in:
    • Detecting equipment malfunctions or breakdowns
    • Monitoring error rates and product defects
    • Tracking downtime and production delays
    Automated triggers ensure that issues are identified promptly, even if operators are not immediately aware of them.

Combining both manual and automated triggers can provide a comprehensive Andon system that maximizes responsiveness and minimizes downtime. Ensure that the triggers are well-integrated with your Andon board and clearly understood by all team members.

5. Implement Visual Controls

Install and integrate the chosen visual indicators with your workspace. Proper implementation ensures that the Andon system is effective and accessible to all team members:

    Lights: Mount Andon lights in locations where they are easily visible to all relevant personnel. Consider the following:
    • Install lights at key points along the production line to ensure visibility from various angles.
    • Use high-mounted lights for large workspaces or areas with obstructions.
    • Ensure lights are color-coded (e.g., green for normal operation, yellow for attention, red for stoppage) and visible from a distance.
    Proper placement of lights helps in quickly signaling the status of operations to everyone on the floor.
    Boards: Set up digital displays or physical boards in strategic locations. These boards should be:
    • Placed at entrances or central points where they can be easily seen by all team members.
    • Large enough to display critical information clearly.
    • Updated in real-time to provide accurate data on the current status of operations.
    Digital boards can be integrated with your systems for automatic updates, while physical boards should be maintained regularly.

Implementing visual controls effectively requires planning and consideration of the workspace layout. Ensure that all team members are trained on how to interpret the visual indicators and respond appropriately. Regular maintenance and updates are essential to keep the Andon system reliable and functional.

  • Providing ongoing training and refreshers to keep everyone up-to-date with procedures and best practices.
Clearly defined responsibilities ensure that there is no confusion during an alert, and that issues are resolved efficiently.

Regular training sessions and drills can help reinforce knowledge and ensure that all personnel are prepared to effectively use the Andon system. Continuous education and practice are key to maintaining a responsive and efficient operational environment.

7. Establish Response Procedures

Develop clear protocols for responding to Andon alerts. Well-defined response procedures ensure that issues are addressed promptly and efficiently:

Escalation: Define who to notify for each level of alert to ensure the right personnel are informed and can take action. Consider the following steps:
  • Identify the specific individuals or roles responsible for responding to different types of alerts.
  • Establish a chain of command for escalating alerts from operators to team leaders, supervisors, and maintenance staff.
  • Ensure that contact information and communication channels (e.g., phone, email, radio) are readily available and regularly updated.
  • Develop criteria for when and how alerts should be escalated to higher levels of management or external support teams.
Clear escalation procedures help in quickly mobilizing the appropriate response and minimizing downtime.
Resolution: Establish standard procedures for addressing and resolving issues promptly. This includes:
  • Creating step-by-step guides for common issues and their resolutions.
  • Ensuring that all necessary tools and resources are available to address problems as they arise.
  • Training personnel on troubleshooting techniques and the use of diagnostic equipment.
  • Implementing a feedback loop to review resolved issues and improve procedures continuously.
  • Documenting each incident, including the actions taken and the outcome, to build a knowledge base for future reference.
Standardized resolution procedures ensure consistency and effectiveness in handling alerts.

Regularly review and update response procedures to adapt to changing operational needs and incorporate lessons learned from past incidents. Effective response protocols are vital for maintaining productivity and ensuring workplace safety.
